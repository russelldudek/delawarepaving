The decision surface

Measure what the work stops costing.

The scorecard should establish baselines before promises. It tracks operating behavior, economics, trust, and adoption - not vanity counts of bots or prompts.

01 · FLOW

Cycle time

How quickly work moves from estimate to ready project, from field event to decision, and from completion to closeout.

Measure: elapsed time and waiting time by handoff
02 · FRICTION

Manual handling

Where people rekey, search, reconcile, chase, recreate, or manually assemble routine evidence.

Measure: touches, rekeying events, labor hours avoided
03 · QUALITY

Completeness

Whether scope, assumptions, approvals, documents, and field evidence are present when downstream teams need them.

Measure: required-field and evidence completeness
04 · CONTROL

Exception age

How long unresolved scope, approval, documentation, or integration exceptions remain without an owner.

Measure: open exceptions by age and accountable owner
05 · ADOPTION

Retained use

Whether the workflow is actually used after launch and makes the job easier enough to displace the workaround.

Measure: active use, completion rate, fallback behavior
06 · RELIABILITY

Production trust

Whether integrations, automations, and AI assistance perform consistently, visibly, securely, and recoverably.

Measure: failures, recovery time, overrides, auditability

First 120 days

Earn the architecture through the work.

Start on-site with estimators, project managers, operations, finance, and the people maintaining the systems. Build a production pattern, not a technology theater.

Days 0-30

Read the system

  • Map three high-value workflows in the room and in the field.
  • Inventory systems, data, integrations, permissions, and shadow work.
  • Baseline cycle time, manual handling, completeness, and exceptions.
  • Define decision rights and production-readiness criteria.
Days 31-60

Build the first joint

  • Pilot the estimate-to-project handoff with a narrow team.
  • Establish one integration, logging, error, and support pattern.
  • Create the minimum useful project workspace and exception review.
  • Instrument adoption and operational outcomes from day one.
Days 61-90

Prove the surface

  • Launch field-evidence or project-visibility workflow.
  • Review failure modes with users and operational leaders.
  • Publish scorecard, runbook, ownership, and recovery path.
  • Retire, revise, or scale based on evidence.
Days 91-120

Form the function

  • Prioritize finance, AR/AP, sales, and enterprise opportunities.
  • Set portfolio cadence, architecture standards, and governance.
  • Define internal hiring, partner, and vendor needs.
  • Turn the first wins into reusable integration and adoption patterns.
Open the detailed 120-day plan
“He has not spent his career inside a paving contractor - and his résumé does not claim NetSuite or Graph API production ownership.”

That is the honest concern. The response is not to blur the gap. It is to show the adjacent pattern: Russell has repeatedly entered complex operating environments, learned the real work, built mechanisms across business and technical boundaries, connected ERP and workflow systems, instrumented the result, and made the new standard usable.

He would begin with construction humility and on-site discovery, not a generic AI playbook. NetSuite, Power Automate, SharePoint, and Graph depth should be tested directly in interview; the first 120 days explicitly pair fast learning with governed integration patterns and accountable operational ownership.

Executive discovery

Questions worth answering before the first build.

Where does awarded work most often lose estimate assumptions, scope boundaries, or supporting evidence before the project team acts?
Which numbers do operational leaders challenge because the source, timing, or ownership is unclear?
What manual step is expensive mainly because it creates downstream exceptions rather than because it consumes minutes?
Which system is treated as authoritative today - and where do people quietly maintain a second truth?
What must remain locally flexible across offices, customers, project types, or field conditions?
What would make estimators and project managers say the first automation genuinely gave time back rather than added a compliance step?
Where should AI assist judgment, and where should deterministic workflow and controls remain the default?
What is the strongest reason a prior systems or process change did not stick?
